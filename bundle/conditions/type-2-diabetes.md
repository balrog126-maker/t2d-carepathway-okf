## Definition

Type 2 diabetes (T2D) results from a progressive, non-autoimmune decline in β-cell insulin secretion against a background of insulin resistance. It accounts for 90–95% of all diabetes. Hyperglycemia develops gradually and may go undiagnosed for years.

## Pathophysiology

- Relative (not absolute) insulin deficiency combined with insulin resistance
- β-Cell dysfunction driven by genetic predisposition, epigenetic changes, inflammation, and metabolic stress
- Obesity — especially central/visceral adiposity — is a major contributor but not universal; individuals without obesity may have excess visceral or ectopic fat
- DKA is uncommon but can occur with severe metabolic stress (infection, MI, cocaine use, glucocorticoids, SGLT2 inhibitors)

## Key Risk Factors

- Overweight/obesity (BMI ≥25 kg/m², or ≥23 kg/m² in Asian ancestry)
- Prediabetes (IFG, IGT, or A1C 5.7–6.4%)
- History of gestational diabetes
- Family history (first-degree relative)
- Sedentary lifestyle
- High-risk racial/ethnic groups (African American, Latino, Native American, Asian American)
- Polycystic ovary syndrome
- Hypertension, dyslipidemia
- Medications: glucocorticoids, statins, thiazides, antipsychotics, some HIV antiretroviral agents

## Remission

Intensive lifestyle intervention and/or metabolic surgery can lead to diabetes remission. Weight loss is a primary driver.

## Special Subtypes

- **Ketosis-prone diabetes:** Occurs predominantly in Black and Hispanic adults; initially presents like T1D with DKA, but insulin requirement is transient
- **Medication-induced:** Glucocorticoids cause insulin resistance (screen with postprandial/random glucose, not fasting); second-generation antipsychotics require baseline and 12–16 week post-initiation screening

## Differentiation from T1D

In adults without classic T2D risk factors or in younger individuals, consider islet autoantibody testing to exclude T1D. See [Type 1 Diabetes](./type-1-diabetes.md) for AABBCC criteria.
