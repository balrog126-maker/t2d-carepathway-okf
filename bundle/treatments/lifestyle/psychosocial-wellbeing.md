## Core Principle

Psychosocial health is inseparable from diabetes management. Unaddressed mental health conditions significantly worsen self-management behaviors, glycemia, and long-term outcomes.

## Mental Health Screening

Screen at initial visit, annually, and with major changes in clinical status or life events:

| Condition | Screening Tool | Frequency |
|-----------|---------------|-----------|
| Depression | PHQ-2 → PHQ-9 if positive | Initial and annual |
| Anxiety | GAD-2 or GAD-7 | Initial and annual |
| Diabetes distress | Problem Areas in Diabetes (PAID) or DDS-2 | Initial and annual |
| Fear of hypoglycemia | Hypoglycemia Fear Survey (HFS) | Initial and annual |
| Disordered eating | Diabetes Eating Problem Survey (DEPS-R) | Initial and annual |
| Cognitive impairment | MMSE, MoCA (older adults) | As indicated |

## Diabetes Distress

Distinct from clinical depression — characterized by emotional burden, regimen distress, interpersonal distress, and physician distress specific to diabetes demands.

- Affects 18–45% of people with diabetes at any given time
- Often missed because it doesn't meet DSM criteria for depression
- PAID and DDS-2 are validated screening tools
- First-line: DSMES, peer support, regimen simplification; referral to behavioral health if severe

## Depression

- Prevalence 2–3× higher than general population
- Bidirectional: depression worsens glycemic control; hyperglycemia worsens mood
- Screen with PHQ-2 (≥2 points → full PHQ-9)
- First-line treatment: psychotherapy (CBT), antidepressants (SSRIs, SNRIs)
- Note: some antidepressants affect weight and glucose; select agents thoughtfully

## Anxiety

- Fear of hypoglycemia is a major barrier to insulin adherence and TIR
- Anxiety about injections, devices, or test results may limit treatment uptake
- CGM with alerts may reduce fear of hypoglycemia when paired with education

## Disordered Eating

- Insulin restriction for weight loss ("diabulimia") — specific to T1D; significantly increases DKA risk and long-term complications
- Binge eating disorder, night eating syndrome, and restrictive eating all common
- Refer to eating disorder specialist or psychologist if suspected

## Sleep Health

- Poor sleep quality → increased insulin resistance → higher A1C
- Obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) is common, especially in T2D with obesity
- OSA screening: Epworth Sleepiness Scale, STOP-BANG
- OSA treatment with CPAP improves insulin sensitivity and may lower BP; modest A1C improvement

## Religious Fasting

Many patients fast for religious observances (Ramadan, Yom Kippur, Lent, etc.).

### Key Risks
- Hypoglycemia (particularly with insulin, SU)
- Hyperglycemia (large meals at break-fast)
- Dehydration (no fluids in some traditions)

### IDF-DAR Risk Calculator
- Stratifies risk as low, moderate, or high based on diabetes type, complications, medication
- Low risk → can fast with education and medication adjustment
- High risk (T1D, T2D on insulin with complications) → discourage fasting or provide intensive monitoring

### Medication Adjustments for Ramadan (T2D)
| Medication | Adjustment |
|-----------|-----------|
| Metformin | Take with Iftar or split dose |
| SU (glipizide, glimepiride) | Reduce dose or switch to lower-risk agent |
| SGLT2i | Monitor hydration; generally safe |
| GLP-1 RA | No adjustment needed |
| Basal insulin | Reduce 15–30%; take at Iftar |
| Premixed insulin | Significant dose restructuring needed; specialist guidance |

## Social Determinants of Health

Address at each visit:
- Food insecurity → refer to food bank, SNAP, RDN for low-cost eating guidance
- Financial barriers to medication → patient assistance programs, formulary alternatives
- Limited health literacy → visual aids, plain-language education materials
- Social isolation → peer support groups, community health workers
