## Mechanism

Metformin primarily suppresses hepatic glucose production (gluconeogenesis) via activation of AMPK and inhibition of mitochondrial complex I. Secondary effects: improved peripheral insulin sensitivity, modest gut-based glucose modulation.

## Efficacy

- A1C reduction: 1–2%
- Fasting glucose reduction: 60–70 mg/dL
- Weight: weight-neutral to modest weight loss (−1 to −2 kg)
- Cardiovascular: UKPDS demonstrated significant CVD mortality reduction in T2D over 10 years
- Does NOT cause hypoglycemia when used in monotherapy
- Inexpensive; widely available generically

## Dosing

| Dose | Formulation | Notes |
|------|-------------|-------|
| Start: 500 mg once or twice daily with meals | Immediate-release (IR) | Minimize GI side effects by starting low |
| Titrate over 4–8 weeks | — | Increase by 500 mg/week |
| Maximum effective: 2,000 mg/day | — | Doses above 2,500 mg add little benefit |
| Extended-release (XR) | Once daily | Better GI tolerability; no evidence of greater efficacy |

## eGFR-Based Dosing Guidance

| eGFR | Action |
|------|--------|
| ≥45 | Continue; no restriction |
| 30–44 | Use with caution; reduce to ≤1,000 mg/day; monitor closely |
| <30 | Contraindicated (increased lactic acidosis risk) |
| Hospitalized patients | Hold if receiving IV contrast; restart 48h post-procedure |

## GI Side Effects

Most common reason for discontinuation (affecting ~20–30%):
- Nausea, diarrhea, abdominal cramping — usually dose-related and transient
- Strategies: start low and titrate slowly; take with food; switch to XR formulation
- Metallic taste (less common)

**Lactic acidosis:** Rare (<10 per 100,000 patient-years); risk primarily in renal impairment, liver failure, or severe hypoxia (contrast caution). Not a concern at recommended doses in normal/mildly impaired renal function.

## Vitamin B12 Deficiency

- Chronic metformin use reduces B12 absorption (via intrinsic factor-Ca2+ mechanism in terminal ileum)
- Prevalence: ~7–30% after >5 years of use
- Check B12 levels annually if on metformin >5 years OR if peripheral neuropathy is present
- Supplement B12 (oral or IM) if low; does not require metformin discontinuation

## Contraindications

- eGFR <30 mL/min/1.73 m²
- Active hepatic impairment or alcohol abuse (lactic acidosis risk)
- Hypersensitivity

## Role in the T2D Algorithm

Metformin remains the preferred **initial** oral agent for T2D because of:
- Proven long-term safety (UKPDS 35+ year follow-up)
- CVD mortality reduction (UKPDS)
- Low hypoglycemia risk
- Low cost and global availability

However, its priority is now context-dependent: patients with ASCVD, HF, or CKD should have GLP-1 RA or SGLT2i considered **independently of A1C** and often first-line or in combination.
