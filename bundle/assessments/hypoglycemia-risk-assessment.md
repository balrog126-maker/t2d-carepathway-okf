## Overview

Hypoglycemia is the main barrier to achieving glycemic targets in insulin-requiring and sulfonylurea-treated patients. Risk assessment at every visit identifies who needs prevention strategies.

## Major Risk Factors

| Risk Factor | Notes |
|-------------|-------|
| Insulin therapy (any type) | Highest-risk medication class |
| Sulfonylurea or meglitinide use | Second-generation SUs carry significant risk |
| Prior hypoglycemia episode | Best predictor of future events |
| Impaired awareness of hypoglycemia (IAH) | Autonomic failure; symptoms blunted at <70 mg/dL |
| Long diabetes duration | More likely to develop IAH over time |
| Tight glycemic control or low A1C | Less margin before hypoglycemia |
| Age (older adults) | Impaired counter-regulation, polypharmacy |
| Renal impairment (eGFR <45) | Reduced insulin clearance, impaired gluconeogenesis |
| Hepatic impairment | Impaired glycogenolysis |
| Alcohol use | Blocks hepatic glucose output; blunts symptoms |
| Nocturnal hypoglycemia | CGM essential for detection |

## Social and Practical Risk Factors

- Living alone without supervision or alert system
- Food insecurity or irregular meal timing
- Lack of CGM access
- Inability to recognize or treat hypoglycemia independently (cognitive impairment)
- Limited English proficiency without educational support
- Cost barriers to glucose-raising supplies (glucagon, glucose tablets)

## Impaired Hypoglycemia Awareness (IAH)

- Also called **hypoglycemia-associated autonomic failure (HAAF)**
- Defined as: diminished or absent adrenergic/neurogenic warning symptoms at glucose <70 mg/dL
- Caused by repeated prior hypoglycemia events → autonomic desensitization
- Confirmed by Gold or Clarke scoring methods (7-question patient survey)
- **Treatment:** Strict hypoglycemia avoidance for 2–3 weeks can partially restore awareness; CGM with alerts strongly recommended

## Assessment at Each Visit

Ask specifically:
1. Have you had any low blood sugar reactions since your last visit?
2. Did you need help from another person to treat any of those lows?
3. Do you notice symptoms (tremor, sweating, palpitations) when your glucose is low, or do you find out only from your meter/CGM?

## Medications That Do NOT Cause Hypoglycemia (in Monotherapy)

- Metformin
- SGLT2 inhibitors
- GLP-1 receptor agonists
- DPP-4 inhibitors
- TZDs

These are preferred in patients with high hypoglycemia risk.

## Prevention Strategies

- Switch from sulfonylurea/insulin to low-hypoglycemia-risk agent if A1C at or below target
- Use CGM with low glucose alerts (<70 mg/dL)
- Use AID (automated insulin delivery) systems in T1D — significantly reduce nocturnal hypoglycemia
- Prescribe glucagon kit for all patients on insulin or high-risk oral agents
- Educate on "15-15 rule" and when to call for help
- Adjust insulin-to-carbohydrate ratios for activity, alcohol, and sick days
